This guide will help you use Genesis: Ember as a teaching tool in your classroom. Each section contains explanations, discussions you can have with your class, and observation activities tied to the game's mechanics.

What is Genesis: Ember?

Genesis: Ember is a browser-based genetics simulation where students manage a population of glowing organisms called Embers inside a virtual petri dish. Through gameplay, students observe genetic inheritance, random drift, population bottlenecks, and the founder effect in real-time.

Learning Outcomes

After reflecting on the game, the students should understand:

  • The Founder Effect: A small founding population carries only a subset of the original gene pool, and that subset defines all future generations.
  • Genetic Drift: Allele frequencies change randomly over generations, independent of fitness or selection.
  • Bottleneck Effect: A sudden population crash reduces genetic diversity dramatically and unpredictably.
  • Epistasis: Some genes mask the expression of others, creating hidden genetic variation.

Target Audience

Designed for high school biology students (ages 15–19), and adaptable as a student activity in an introductory university genetics courses.

Suggested Play Time

One session: 15 minutes for a single game mode.
All sessions: 60 - 90 minutes to cover all modes with guided discussion.

Before Playing: Foundational Concepts

The game goes beyond Mendel: alleles blend, genes mask each other, and drift does the rest

These are the core biology concepts students will encounter during gameplay. Consider introducing them briefly before students play, or use this section as a reference during discussion.

Alleles & Diploidy

An allele is a specific variation of a gene's DNA sequence. Most organisms we commonly think of are diploid, meaning they carry two alleles for each gene, one inherited from each parent. The physical traits we see are rarely the result of a "winner" or "loser" allele. Instead, traits emerge from a nuanced blend or specific expression.

[ In the game ] Each ember is diploid: it carries two colour alleles. Click any ember to see its alleles in the info panel (e.g. crimson + gold).

Genotype vs Phenotype

The genotype is the set of genes an organism carries in its DNA, the blueprint. The phenotype is the observable expression of those genes, representing what you actually see. Think of it as:

Genotype + Environment = Phenotype

[ In the game ] An ember's visible colour (phenotype) is a blend of its two colour alleles (genotype). Two embers that look similar might carry very different allele combinations, and vice versa.

Incomplete Dominance

In incomplete dominance, neither allele fully masks the other. Instead, each gene "gives a little," resulting in a blend between the traits. The phenotype is a mix proportional to each allele's expression strength.

[ In the game ] Each allele has a strength value (0.0–1.0). The ember's final colour is a weighted blend of both alleles' colours. A crimson (0.8) + gold (0.3) ember will appear more red than yellow.
⚠ Not the same as co-dominance. In co-dominance, both alleles express fully and side by side (like blood type AB, where both A and B are present). In incomplete dominance, the result is a single blended trait. The game uses incomplete dominance.

Gene Pool

A gene pool is the combination of all genes (including all allele variants) present in a reproducing population. A large gene pool means extensive genetic diversity and a population that is better able to withstand environmental challenges. A small gene pool makes populations less able to adapt.

[ In the game ] The allele frequency panel on the right side of the screen shows your population's gene pool. Watch how it changes over time, as this is the foundation for everything that follows.

Genetic Drift

Genetic drift is a random fluctuation in the frequency of an allele in a population. It has nothing to do with fitness or selection, but rather it is pure chance. Drift is strongest in small populations, and can cause traits to become overwhelmingly frequent or to disappear entirely.

[ In the game ] Even without any player intervention, allele proportions shift randomly from generation to generation. Both the founder effect and bottleneck events result in genetic drift.

Population Bottleneck

A bottleneck occurs when something eliminates a large part of a population (a natural disaster, an epidemic), resulting in a new allele frequency. The survivors carry only a fraction of the original genetic diversity, and that reduced diversity defines all future generations.

[ In the game ] Germ outbreaks and virus epidemics can kill large portions of the population quickly. After the event, check the allele panel and notice how many alleles may have been lost.
⚠ A bottleneck is not the same as natural selection. The survivors are not necessarily "fitter", they simply happened to survive.

The Founder Effect

The founder effect occurs when a small group of individuals becomes separated from a larger population and establishes a new one. The founders carry only a subset of the original gene pool. Over time, the new population's genotypes and traits will resemble those of the small founding group, which may be very different from the original population.

It is a random sample, meaning the founders do not necessarily represent the original population they came from.

[ In the game ] After the tutorial, the dish is reset and 10 new random embers are spawned. These 10 founders determine every allele that will ever exist in the population unless mutation occurs.

Epistasis

Epistasis occurs when the expression of one gene is dependent on the presence of one or more "modifier" genes. A classic example is albinism, where an organism might carry all the alleles for dark brown eyes, but if it carries the epistatic gene for albinism, those eye colour genes are masked completely.

[ In the game ] Every ember carries a hidden flicker gene. When its strength is high enough, it can turn the flicker gene on, which in turn zeroes out one colour channel (red, green, or blue), which produces unexpected colours regardless of the ember's colour alleles. This is epistasis, the phenomenon of one gene masking the expression of another.

Game Modes Overview

Genesis: Ember features several directed teaching modes. Each mode is accessed by entering a specific keyword (Class Code) on the start screen. If the keyword is left blank, the game starts in free play mode.

In all directed modes, the game begins with a short tutorial. After the tutorial concludes, the screen dips to black and the player is presented with the mode's specific goal. The dish is cleared, and 10 new random embers spawn, practically demonstrating the founder effect.

Fixation Mode

CLASS CODE 
Keyword:fixation

Keyword: fixation

Goal: Achieve fixation by ensuring all living embers (minimum 10) carry the exact same colour allele.

What it teaches: You are the selector, and diversity is the default. To achieve fixation, students must actively cull all competing alleles from the gene pool.

Diversity Mode (Genotype)

CLASS CODE 
Keyword: genotype

Keyword: genotype

Goal: Maintain at least 20 instances of each founding allele in the gene pool simultaneously.

What it teaches: Diversity is fragile. Students must carefully manage the population to prevent genetic drift from eliminating rare alleles. They must work with what the founder effect gave them.

Directed Selection Mode (Founding)

CLASS CODE 
Keyword: founding

Keyword: founding

Goal: Grow a randomly designated target allele to 50 instances without letting any other founding allele drop to zero.

What it teaches: Directed selection has trade-offs. Favouring one trait heavily can inadvertently cause a bottleneck for others. Students learn they are constrained by their starting conditions.

Epistasis Mode (Flicker)

CLASS CODE 
Keyword: flicker

Keyword: flicker

Goal: Fulfill 5 specific orders that require embers expressing the flicker gene.

What it teaches: Epistasis is heritable and can be selected for. Because the flicker gene masks the underlying genotype, breeding for it requires careful observation of inheritance patterns.

After Playing: Discussion & Reflection

Don't rush to give them the answers! Let the students debate why a colour vanished.
Validating their frustration makes the transition from 'game' to 'learning' much easier.

Use these questions to prompt class discussion after students have completed the game modes:

Observation & Extinction

  • "What happened to the population's colors over time? Did any colors disappear? Why do you think that happened?"
  • "If a specific color was missing from the 10 founding embers at the start of a mode, could that allele ever appear later? What does this tell us about the founder effect?"

Facilitating Discussion

Expect students to feel overwhelmed. The game is intentionally chaotic, populations will crash, and random events will interfere. This is a feature, not a bug. Use this frustration as a teaching moment. Remind students that real populations aren't static, they are dynamic, and will constantly change due to random events, and that is normal.

  • "Did you feel like you had control? Why or why not?"
  • "What does this tell us about real populations facing environmental challenges (bottlenecks)?"
  • "How does it feel when diversity is lost and you can't get it back?"

Reference Materials

Historical Figures

Gregor Mendel (1822–1884)
A monk, gardener, and the father of modern genetics who crossbred and purebred peas for many years. He observed that he could predict the ratio of traits in later generations. He saw that traits were passed down from parents to their offspring, and called them "hereditary factors". While Genesis: Ember explores more complex inheritance, Mendel's work laid the absolute foundation.

Wilhelm Johannsen (1857–1927)
A Danish pharmacist, botanist, plant physiologist, and geneticist. He coined the core terminology we still use today, and which you use in this game, including the terms gene, phenotype, and genotype.
